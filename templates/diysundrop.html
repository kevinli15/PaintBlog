DiY Sundrop

: January 17th 2021 (UPDATED: Jan 21st, 2021)    : 0   

Left: DiY                                   Right: Official

If you’re not here for an explanation of the DiY Sundrop method, it comes down to this: prime in black, dry brush on the darkest color, dry brush on a lighter color, then one last layer of dry brush in the lightest color. Simple and easy; in and out in less than five minutes per mini (minus primer drying time). Otherwise let’s begin.


Required Materials
  • 2 Paintbrushes (One is the dedicated Primer brush)
  • Black Primer (Can be substituted with black acrylic paint, see update)
  • Three colors (explained below)
  • Water
  • Protective Spray
(I hope I don't get paint on my boxes...)

Introduction

It’s been confirmed by Awaken Realms that the official Sundrop method uses washes, which we will not being employing. Washes tend to be expensive as well as complicate the painting process. Instead we will be employing dry brushing on black primer, anywhere between two to four (or more if you prefer) layers of dry brush to simulate the Sundrop effect.

The official Sundrop is on the right on both photos, wash is noticeable but is it worth the trouble and money?

This is an extremely fast and efficient way of painting minis. It’s become my default for when I don’t have the time or patience to paint tons of miniatures. For example, the as of yet unreleased Oathsworn contains the Armory box, which has tons of little weapons. I will be employing this DiY Sundrop technique to Oathsworn when I receive it (as well as Nemesis, Vanguard, Great Wall...) as painting those normally would be very difficult and time consuming.

Blood Rage on the left, Wasteland Express on the right. All done using the same method!

This method also creates a great base coat, ready to be painted on if you decide to go with more details:

DiY Sundrop result, just add a few more colors!

Color Selection

This guide will use Tainted Grail as an example but again, this method can be used on any game. The only difference is the colors you use. The colors you pick all should be somewhat close to each other. In our example I’m trying to emulate the official Tainted Grail Sundrops, which look like a mix of brown and beige. So I chose a darker brown, a lighter brown and an off white. The darkest color should be your first coat of paint. Then every subsequent layer should be lighter in tone. Note that my off white isn’t going to be directly applied; it will be mixed with my other browns to lighten them up. Getting a lighter tone of a color is easily done by mixing that color with white or a brighter color:

All that's needed

I. Priming your Mini

UPDATE (Jan 21st, 2021): You can substitute black primer with black paint if you wish, see photos at bottom of page for comparison.

If you haven’t designated a permanent primer brush I recommend doing so. Primer is thick and will ruin your brushes in the long run. It should be a smaller brush so you can get into crevices, but a large brush will save time on larger minis. Do both if you have enough brushes!

Spread and cover every millimeter in primer

Wet your brush, apply primer to it and start painting your mini. You’ll probably have to stop half way through priming as you’ll need dry places to hold the primer as you prime your mini 360°. Spread the primer out as fully as you can, you don’t want it bunched up. A little bunching is OK since primer will dry. Some crevices are very difficult to spread thinly as your brush may be too big, but as long as you get the primer in there, even bunched up primer should dry fine. When youre waiting for your primer to dry, be sure to clean your brushes under the sink to prolong their life. Primer will glue your bristles together!

The left photo shows primer bunching, the right photo should be the end result after you spread thoroughly with your brush

A small paint brush will save your sanity in crevices like here

Double check all crevices for easily missed spots. Change your room’s lighting if you need to get a better view. Wait for your figure to fully dry before taking the next step. The drying in between can take anywhere between 30 minutes to an hour.

Missed a spot

II. Dry brushing the first layer

Dry brushing is a technique which involves a dry paint brush with a minimal amount of paint, applied to the miniature in a quick smothering like motion. The motion is similar to when Bob Ross use to “beat the devil” out of his brushes.

Pour out your darkest color. For me it was this dark brown which ended being too dark so I mixed in some off white to lighten it up. Thoroughly mix to get one color.

Delicious.

Dip the tip of your second, dry paintbrush in, making sure to cover the tips of all the bristles. I like using a larger brush here since the larger size helps spread paint more efficiently. Now run your brush along a paper towel or newspaper to remove excess paint. The rule of thumb with dry brushing is that when you wipe your paintbrush across a paper towel and you still see paint being painted on the towel, there is still too much paint on the brush. This step is extremely important as too much paint will cause your mini to not come out right. I would say over 90% of the paint should be off the brush when you're removing paint from your dry brush. Also when running your brush across your paper towel, brush in all directions! I can almost guarantee there will be pockets of paint that are hidden and you want those removed!

Keep wiping until your brush is barely making paint marks

Now just apply the motion mentioned above to your mini. You want your swipes to be quick; back and forth. As a side note if you haven’t used a color or brand of paint before, I suggest you paint a small amount on your brush and apply to the figure just to see how it looks. In general painting on top of a dark base will produce a darker layer on top. Another issue you may have is your paint not sticking at all:

2 examples of this brand of paint not sticking, those photos were taken after applying paint!

When your paint works correctly, you will see an immediate effect:

All the details should pop out immediately. Note the shield in photo 2, a sign of too much paint

You can see above the mini brightens up and all the detail start to come through. Look out for what happened in the second photo which is too much paint. Your dry brushing should result in a light coat of paint hitting all the details and crevices. Photo two is an indication that not enough paint was removed from the brush during the previous step. You can try wiping the paint off your mini if this happens, or in more extreme circumstances, wipe off the paint with something wet. You can accidentally remove your primer by doing this but no worries, just re-prime. Either way wait until your mini is dry before you try again.

III. Dry brushing the other layer(s)

From here on out you’re just dry brushing additional layers. Every layer will be lighter in tone than the one before. Every application of that paint will be applied to your mini in less time than before. For example if the first layer of dry brush was applied over 15 seconds, the 2nd layer could by applied over 8 seconds, third layer would only be 5 seconds. Go by how it looks to determine the time. The number of layers you want is up to you. Between two to four is good, I did three for this figure.

More off-white to my paint from the layer before to get a lighter color. Get around 95% of the paint off before you dry brush the 2nd layer!

A second layer of dry brush applied, more lightly than the first layer and applied more quickly

Seeing as the lighter brown hasn't been used at all, I figured to use him for the last layer so he wouldn't have his feelings hurt. Joking aside, it's good having different colors as that will create different subtle effects, so I encourage you try different colors rather than just using once color and adding white to lighten it for every layer. I again mixed with off white to bring the mixture to a lighter color tone than the already applied paint. Here the dry brushing is extremely light and only hitting the most outward parts of the mini. Weapons, foreheads, shoulders are almost always what you're aiming for as you get on your last layers. Thank of sunlight shining down. For me, the dry brushing applications at the last layer is barely a few swipes of the paintbrush.

A third and final layer of dry brush applied

It may be hard to see in these photos the effects of each layer but I assure you makes a difference!

IV. Protective Spray

The last step is protecting your figure. Be outside or in a well-ventilated area. Give your spray can a good shake and be one foot to two feet away from your mini and spray. Don’t spray too close or you figure will get glossy! This was a mistake I learned early on. You may have to spray in layers if you can’t get 360° around your mini. I also recommend spraying in quick short bursts, this will help you control how much spray is being applied as well as overspraying which will result in a glossy texture. Let the varnish dry before returning, rotating or flipping your mini and spray again.

Be in a well-ventilated area, inhaling this stuff is probably bad for you


Right guy's varnish applied badly. He looks like a swimmer

Just like that you're done! It's still amazing how little work it takes to make an unpainted mini look decent. Once you have the technique down it just comes to color selection. For Nemesis for instance, i think dark blue or silver might be good. Just experiment and have fun!

UPDATE (Jan 21st, 2021)

I decided to try black acrylic paint instead of black primer to see if there was any difference.

Left: The dude we're testing on Right: The giant is black primer vs the guy in black paint. Giants's black is sightly duller

The finished product, no major differences between this and primer as base

My conclusion? I didn't notice any major differences, substitute away. Keep in mind though black primer does have other uses where black acrylic paint may not be able to substitute, but for this example it would appear fine. I'll update if I notice anything different with the figure as time passes.
