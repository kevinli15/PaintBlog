Materials Buying Guide

January 16th 2021


Buying materials when you first start painting can certainly be overwhelming; a simple search on Amazon for miniatures paint sets can return products that price into triple figures. On top of that there’s tons of terminology out there which again and put off a fledgling painter, making the hobby seem more intimidating than it really is.

Just the basics

However the beauty of any art is that it’s more about the painter rather than the paintbrush. Your skill and techniques are ultimately going to be the most important tool of all, so don't worry so much about the quality of materials you buy. You can easily get the basic required materials for under $40, which will last you many hundreds of minis.

Required Materials

  • Brush Set
  • Black primer
  • Set of acrylic paints
  • Protective finish spray

Yup, that's it. I would say about 90% of the figures I've painted use only the above. The DiY Sundrop requires no washes at all while the Rising Sun figures clan figures are washed with a premade wash. Let's go into a quick summary of each item:

No wash required!

Homemade wash

Brush Set

I use a lot of a technique called dry brushing, which is the process of applying a small amount of paint on a dry brush and quickly brushing it across the figure. Dry brushing is AMAZING for painting as when done in the right context it can act as a substitute for expensive washes. However dry brushing has also been known to damage brushes, which is why it's a common recommendation to buy cheaper brushes. Make up brushes are great because to dry brush you need more bristles which make up brushes have in supply. Avoid sets from dollar stores because their cheap materials. Aim for sets that have thin and thick brushes that are priced between $5 to $10.

Clean after every paint session!

(Black) Primer

Think of primer as a pre-paint paint. It’s the layer that goes on first to help your actual paint stick. The interesting thing about primer is that whatever color your primer is, it can have a huge effect on your figure. For example, figures that have a white or lighter primer will be brighter overall, whereas black primer which create a darker tone. Some colors like yellow infamously won’t appear correctly when applied to black primer because of how the thinner, lighter yellow reacts to a dark base color. I use black primer because dry brushing on black primer immediately accentuates all of a figure’s details. I’d go even as far to say that if you prime in black and then dry brush just one layer on top, you could call it a day and your figures would still look great. I specifically use heavy black gesso as my primer.

UPDATE (Jan 21st, 2021) I've been exprimenting with just regular black arylic paint rather than black primer, see the results at the bottom of this article.

Acrylic Paint

A set of basic Acrylic paints will get you pretty far. Try to get matte and avoid gloss until you have more experience. Remember you can always mix to get certain colors. I recommend getting a larger size black and white paint as those are commonly used to darken/lighten your paint. Also try a brand of acrylic paints before committing to a full set. Also if you aren't buying the more expesnive miniatures specifc paints, generic arylic paint can sometimes have an issues such as not sticking to your figures (don’t buy these).

Under a $1 a bottle

Protective Spray

Sealer, varnish, protective finish…whatever you want to call it I think this is absolutely required. Protecting your hard work is pretty simple considering a scratched figure can be a headache to fix.

All paint chips, even officially painted ones!

Optional Materials
  • Wash
  • Metal/Bronze/Gold Paint
  • White/Grey Primer
  • Paint Remover
Wash

Washes are amazing and can instantly and easily take a mini up a few tiers when used. Despite their strengths however, I would say to hold off on purchasing because they are expensive. A lot of the techniques I employ don't require it at all and when I do need it, I usually just mix my own (though the quality is so-so). Certain methods use wash, others don't, buy only if you know you need it.

Metal/Bronze/Gold Paint

Armor, weapons, guns, treasures, pipes, the list goes on and on. The shiny effect can't really be replicated with regular paints. Grey and yellow paints can substitute for metal and gold, but if you see the real deal, pick it up.

Very versatile

White/Grey Primer

I'm not the biggest fan of using lighter primers because the dry bushing techniques I use has a very different effect on the mini. That being said a lighter primer is much easier to work with if you are just painting regularly. I sometimes just paint directly (no primer) on the figure when I'm not dry brushing or even just use white paint as a primer. In my experience also, white primer doesn't stick as well on minis, often with the plastic showing through.

Multiple white primers I've tried

Paint Remover

Everyone makes mistakes, it's good to have something to erase those mistakes. I use LA Totally Awesome which only runs for a few dollars and can be found at retail stores (at least in the U.S. anyways). Check out this video for a great comparison test.
